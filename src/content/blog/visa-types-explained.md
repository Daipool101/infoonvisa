Before you book a single flight, one question decides how much lead time your trip needs: **what kind of visa (if any) do you need?** Almost every country–to–country route falls into one of four buckets. Once you can tell them apart, the whole planning process gets simpler.

Here's a plain-English breakdown of each — with the practical details that actually matter.

## 1. Visa-free

**What it means:** You can enter with just a valid passport. No application, no fee, no embassy visit.

Visa-free access is usually granted for short tourist stays (often 30, 60, or 90 days) under agreements between countries. You'll still pass through immigration, and an officer can ask for proof of onward travel or funds — but there's no visa to arrange in advance.

**Watch out for:**
- A **maximum stay limit** — overstaying, even by a day, can mean fines or future entry bans.
- **Passport validity rules** — many countries require 6 months of validity beyond your travel dates.
- Some "visa-free" entries still require a **free online travel authorization** (see ETA below).

## 2. Visa on arrival (VOA)

**What it means:** You get the visa at the airport or land border **when you arrive**, rather than applying beforehand.

You typically fill a short form, pay a fee, and get a stamp or sticker on the spot. It's convenient, but it isn't risk-free.

**Watch out for:**
- **Carry the exact requirements** — a passport photo, the fee in local currency or USD cash, a return ticket, and a hotel booking are commonly requested.
- **Queues can be long**, especially at busy airports.
- VOA eligibility can change at short notice — always confirm it still applies to your passport before you fly.

## 3. e-Visa / ETA (apply online before you travel)

**What it means:** You apply through an **official government website** before your trip and receive an electronic approval by email.

- An **e-Visa** is a full visa issued digitally.
- An **ETA** (Electronic Travel Authorization) is a lighter pre-screening that visa-free travelers sometimes need (for example, for the US, Canada, or parts of Europe).

This is the most common modern category, and the most important rule is simple: **apply only on the official portal.** Many lookalike sites charge extra "service" fees for the same government process.

**Watch out for:**
- **Processing time** — usually a few days, but leave a buffer in case of delays.
- **Validity window** — your e-Visa often must be *used* within a set period from issue.
- **Entry conditions** — single vs multiple entry, and the maximum stay per visit.

## 4. Embassy / consulate visa (apply in person)

**What it means:** You submit a full application — often in person at an embassy, consulate, or an authorized visa center (VFS, BLS, etc.) — with documents, photos, and sometimes an interview.

This is the most involved category and applies to many "high-scrutiny" routes (for example, Schengen visas, US B1/B2, or UK standard visitor visas for several nationalities).

**Watch out for:**
- **Start early.** Appointment slots and processing can take weeks, especially in peak season.
- **Documents matter most.** Proof of funds, employment, accommodation, a day-wise itinerary, and confirmed tickets are typical. Incomplete or inconsistent paperwork is the leading cause of delays and refusals.
- **Fees are usually non-refundable**, whether or not the visa is granted.

## A quick pre-trip checklist

Whatever category your route falls into, these apply almost everywhere:

1. **Passport valid 6+ months** beyond your return date, with blank pages.
2. **Proof of onward or return travel** — immigration officers often ask.
3. **Proof of funds and accommodation** for the length of your stay.
4. **Travel insurance** — required for some visas, smart for all trips.
5. **Verify on the official source** and note the date you checked, because rules change.
