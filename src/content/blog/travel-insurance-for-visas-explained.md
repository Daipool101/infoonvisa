Travel insurance is the requirement people treat as optional — right up until it gets their otherwise-perfect application refused. For some destinations it's genuinely mandatory, with **specific minimum coverage**, and a policy that falls short is an instant rejection ground.

Here's exactly what's required, and how to get it right.

## When is insurance mandatory vs. optional?

**Mandatory (part of the visa rules):**
- The **[Schengen area](/blog/schengen-visa-explained)** is the big one — travel medical insurance is compulsory, with a **minimum €30,000** in medical coverage, valid across all Schengen countries for your entire stay.
- Several other countries require proof of medical cover for entry (this changes over time — check your route page).

**Strongly recommended (not required, but wise):**
- Everywhere else. Medical care abroad can cost a fortune; even where no embassy asks for it, travelling uninsured is a gamble.

The rule of thumb: **if insurance is mandatory, treat it as a pass/fail document. If it's optional, treat it as protecting your wallet.**

## What the policy must actually cover

For visa purposes, officers look for:

- **Medical emergencies and hospitalisation** up to the required minimum (€30,000 for Schengen)
- **Emergency medical evacuation and repatriation**
- **Coverage for the entire duration** — from your entry date to your exit date, ideally with a day or two of buffer
- **Valid in the destination/region** — a Schengen policy must cover the whole Schengen area, not just one country

Nice-to-haves that protect *you* (not required for the visa): trip cancellation, lost baggage, flight delays, adventure-activity cover.

## The insurance mistakes that cause refusals

1. **Coverage below the minimum** — €25,000 for a Schengen visa fails. It must be €30,000+.
2. **Dates that don't cover the full trip** — a policy ending the day before your return flight is a reject.
3. **Wrong region** — buying cover for one country when you need the whole Schengen zone.
4. **Buying it too late** — some consulates want to see the policy at submission; don't leave it to the last day.
5. **A quote instead of a policy** — you usually need an actual issued certificate, not a price estimate.

## How to buy it the smart way

- **Check the exact requirement on your route page first** — coverage amount and whether it's mandatory differ by destination.
- **Match the policy dates to your visa/travel window**, with a small buffer.
- **Keep the certificate as a PDF** — you'll attach it to the application and may be asked for it again at the border.
- **Don't over-buy.** For a visa you need medical cover to the required minimum; expensive "premium" plans aren't more likely to get you approved.
- If your visa is refused and the trip is cancelled, check whether your policy is **refundable** — many visa-linked policies are.

## A quick caution on cost claims

We deliberately **don't quote insurance prices or recommend specific insurers** — prices vary by age, destination, duration and provider, and change constantly. Compare reputable providers yourself, and make sure the policy meets the coverage rules on your route page.
