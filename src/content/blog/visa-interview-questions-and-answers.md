Not every country interviews tourist applicants — many decide purely on your paperwork. But where an interview *is* required (the US is the most famous example), it's often short, and a handful of questions decide the outcome. The good news: interviewers ask the same things, and once you understand **what they're really checking**, the answers become obvious.

The golden rule: **be honest, be brief, and be consistent with your application.** Officers interview hundreds of people; rehearsed-sounding speeches and answers that contradict your forms are the fastest way to a refusal.

## About your trip

**1. Why are you travelling to [country]?**
State your real purpose simply — "tourism, to see Tokyo and Kyoto for 10 days." Don't over-explain.

**2. How long will you stay?**
Match your answer to your itinerary and bookings exactly.

**3. Have you been there before? Where else have you travelled?**
A clean travel history helps. Mention prior trips honestly — they build credibility.

**4. Who are you travelling with?**
Solo, family, friends — just be truthful and consistent with any co-applicants.

## About money

**5. How will you fund this trip?**
"My own savings" / "my salary" — and it must match your [proof of funds](/blog/proof-of-funds-for-tourist-visa). If someone sponsors you, say so and be ready to explain the relationship.

**6. What do you do for work? What's your salary?**
Answer plainly. This ties directly to whether your trip is affordable and whether you'll return.

**7. How much will the trip cost?**
Have a rough, realistic number in mind that fits your budget and bookings.

## About ties to home (the real test)

Most refusals hinge on one hidden question: *will you come back?* These probe that:

**8. Are you employed / studying / running a business?**
Your anchor to home. Bring an employment letter with approved leave, enrolment proof, or business papers.

**9. Are you married? Do you have children?**
Family responsibilities are strong ties — answer honestly.

**10. Do you own property or have other commitments?**
Anything that pulls you home counts.

**11. Do you have relatives in [destination country]?**
Answer truthfully. Having relatives isn't disqualifying, but hiding it (and being caught) is fatal.

## Trickier ones

**12. Have you ever been refused a visa — anywhere?**
**Always say yes if true.** It's on record. Explain briefly and factually. Lying converts a refusal into a misrepresentation ban.

**13. What will you do if your visa is rejected?**
Stay calm: "I'll review the reason and reapply if appropriate." Don't get emotional.

**14. Do you intend to work or study there?**
For a tourist visa the answer is a clear "No."

**15. What's your plan / itinerary day by day?**
Know the outline of your own trip. Vagueness signals a fake purpose.

**16–18. Curveballs** — "Why not a cheaper country?", "Who's looking after your home/kids?", "What's your monthly income vs. this trip's cost?" — all still testing funds + ties. Answer honestly and consistently.

## How to prepare (a short checklist)

- ✅ **Know your own application** — re-read it the night before; your spoken answers must match it
- ✅ **Carry your documents organised** — funds, employment, bookings, itinerary
- ✅ **Dress neatly, arrive early, be polite** — first impressions count
- ✅ **Answer only what's asked** — short answers beat rambling
- ✅ **Never lie** — especially about past refusals or relatives abroad
- ❌ Don't memorise a script · Don't argue · Don't guess numbers you don't know

## The bigger picture

An interview is just one gate — the same fundamentals that win the interview win the whole application: clear purpose, solid funds, and strong ties.
