If Europe is on your list, one word dominates your planning: **Schengen**. Understand it once, and trip planning for most of the continent becomes dramatically simpler — because **one visa application can unlock 29 countries**.

Here's the whole system, explained without jargon.

## What is the Schengen area?

The Schengen area is a group of **29 European countries that abolished border controls between each other**. Once you're inside, you can drive from Paris to Rome to Barcelona without showing your passport at a single border.

It includes most EU members — France, Germany, Italy, Spain, the Netherlands, and more — plus non-EU countries like **Switzerland, Norway and Iceland**.

Two important exceptions trip people up:

- **The United Kingdom and Ireland are NOT in Schengen.** A Schengen visa gets you nowhere at London Heathrow — the UK has [its own visa system](/india-to-united-kingdom).
- Being in the EU ≠ being in Schengen, and vice versa. Cyprus (EU) applies its own rules; Switzerland (not EU) is fully Schengen.

## Who needs a Schengen visa?

It depends entirely on your passport:

- **Visa-exempt nationalities** (Americans, British, Japanese, Singaporeans and others) can visit for up to 90 days without a visa. From late 2026, most will need **ETIAS** — a cheap online travel authorization, similar to the US ESTA — but that's an online form, not a visa.
- **Visa-required nationalities** (including Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi, Sri Lankan, Filipino, and many more) must obtain a **short-stay "Type C" Schengen visa** before travelling.

## The 90/180 rule (the part everyone gets wrong)

A short-stay Schengen visa allows **up to 90 days within any 180-day period** — counted across the *entire* Schengen area, not per country.

The window is **rolling**: on any given day, look back 180 days and count the days you were inside Schengen. That total must never exceed 90. Two weeks in France in March and ten weeks in Spain from June would breach it — even though no single trip felt long.

Overstaying even briefly is recorded in the shared **entry/exit system** and can jeopardise every future European application.

## Visiting multiple countries: where do you apply?

You must apply at the consulate of your **main destination** — the country where you'll spend the most nights. If your nights are evenly split, apply to the country of **first entry**.

Getting this wrong is a real refusal ground. If your itinerary says 6 nights in Italy and 3 in France, the application belongs to Italy — even if you land in Paris.

## Documents you'll need

The core Schengen tourist checklist is consistent across member states:

- Passport valid **3+ months beyond your departure from Schengen**, issued within the last 10 years, 2 blank pages
- Completed application form + recent photos to Schengen spec
- **Travel medical insurance with at least €30,000 coverage**, valid across the whole Schengen area for your full stay — this is mandatory, not optional
- Round-trip flight reservation and accommodation for the entire itinerary
- [Proof of funds](/blog/proof-of-funds-for-tourist-visa) — statements, income evidence
- Employment/ties evidence (leave letter, business registration, enrolment)

Each consulate adds its own specifics — always work from the official checklist linked on your route page.

## Timing and validity

- You can apply up to **6 months before travel**; the official processing standard is around **15 calendar days**, but appointment backlogs at busy consulates can add weeks before you even submit. Apply early.
- First-time applicants usually receive a visa matching their itinerary. Frequent, compliant travellers can receive **multi-year, multiple-entry visas** over time — one more reason never to overstay.

## Common Schengen-specific refusal traps

1. **Insurance that doesn't meet the €30,000 minimum** or doesn't cover the full window
2. **Applying to the wrong country** for your itinerary
3. **"Consulate shopping"** — applying to whichever consulate seems easier; itinerary and application must match
4. The same universal issues that sink any application — see [why visa applications get rejected](/blog/why-visa-applications-get-rejected)
